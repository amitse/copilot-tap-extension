20 Interpretations of "Tap"

20 independent agents, 20 unique metaphors, 20 SVG icons — each answering one question: what does "tap" mean to you?

The app is called tap — a Copilot CLI extension that adds background event filtering and injection. It lets you "tap into" streams of data, filter noise, and surface important signals into your coding session. But "tap" is a word with many meanings. We asked 20 agents to each bring their own.

The Brief

What we needed before exploring

Two artifacts, for the same product. Both have to feel like the same idea.

  1. 01 A 64 × 64 icon. An SVG mark for the Copilot CLI extension card. One size, one strong rendering — we don't need to scale down for very small contexts.
  2. 02 A terminal glyph. One to three characters — ASCII or Unicode — that prefix every message tap injects into the CLI. Must render in monospace fonts (Cascadia, Fira, Consolas), stay visually distinct from prompt markers like $ ❯ ▶, and connect semantically to the icon.

The icon brands the product. The glyph attaches to every line it speaks. They have to be the same idea in two media.

Brainstorming — 20 Agents, 20 Metaphors

Each agent answered the same question independently: what does "tap" mean to you?

The brief was deliberately open. Pick a metaphor. Design the icon. Propose a glyph. We wanted variety, not consensus — to see which metaphors held up at icon sizes and in monospace text, and which collapsed when one of those constraints squeezed.

Recurring Themes

Filtering. The strongest cluster — funnel, sieve, prism, hourglass — all different shapes for the same idea: separating signal from noise. This is the core function of the extension.

Monitoring. Wire tap, watchman, sonar, antenna, heartbeat — the extension as a silent observer. It watches so you don't have to, and only speaks when something changes.

Flow control. Water tap, pipeline valve, stream fork — the extension as a mechanism that controls what passes through. You adjust the EventFilter like turning a valve.

Gentle interruption. Finger tap, smart bell, ripple — the extension as a polite notifier. It doesn't scream; it taps your shoulder.

Extraction. Maple tap, tree tap, strike mark — the extension reaches into raw sources and pulls out what's valuable. Most of the source is noise; the extract is signal.

Convergence — Why ※

After 20 explorations, three patterns showed up across the strongest entries.

Diagonal strokes carry intersection energy. The strike, the prism splitting, the wire tap branching off — all read as "something crossed paths here."

Cardinal dots read as captured signals at any size. Sonar ping, strike mark, sieve. Four points fix a moment in space.

No literal metaphor survived as a single character in a terminal. Faucets, hourglasses, antennas — they need too much detail to read at 12px monospace. Only the abstract marks scaled.

The ※ Reference Mark (U+203B) already encodes the first two: an X with four cardinal dots, in one Unicode codepoint. It renders in every monospace font. It literally means "look here, this matters." The icon and the glyph become the same character — which is what we needed all along.

Iteration 12 (Strike Mark) was the closest precursor: same X, same four dots. The convergence wasn't a leap — it was recognizing what the strongest exploration had already found.

Gen 21 — The ※ Reference Mark

One character. Ten icons.

After 20 metaphor explorations, the identity converged on a single Unicode character: ※ (U+203B, Reference Mark). It's perfect — it literally means "look here, this is a reference to something important." That's exactly what tap does: it references the important events from background noise.

The ※ shape is an X — two diagonal strokes — with dots at the four cardinal positions (top, bottom, left, right), nestled between the arms. The diagonals are the streams being monitored. The dots are the signals captured at each compass point.

The mark says: "I noticed something. Look here."

Why ※ works for tap:

It literally means "reference mark" — a typographic symbol that says "look here, this matters." That's exactly what an injected event does. The X strokes are the streams being monitored from all angles. The four cardinal dots are signals captured between the streams — one for each EventFilter outcome: drop, keep, surface, inject. In the CLI, it's just ※ tap: — distinctive, compact, and impossible to confuse with anything else.
